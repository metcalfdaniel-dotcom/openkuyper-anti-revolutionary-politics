# Translator's Notes — Foreword

> **Companion document to the English Foreword of *Antirevolutionaire Staatkunde*.**
> This page records the translation philosophy, lexical decisions, and editorial conventions governing the English rendering. It is written to be pushable as a standalone Markdown file alongside the translation itself.

## 1. Source, scope, and edition

- **Source text.** [Abraham Kuyper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Kuyper), *Antirevolutionaire Staatkunde, met nadere toelichting op "Ons Program"*, Eerste Deel: *De Beginselen* (Kampen: J. H. Kok, 1916), "Woord Vooraf," pp. v–xii.
- **Relationship to prior work.** The Foreword explicitly situates *Staatkunde* as the mature successor to *Ons Program* (1878) and its *Toelichting* / *Nadere Toelichting* (1879). Kuyper had wanted to retain the modest title *Nadere Toelichting*; he was overruled. That editorial history is internal to the text and is preserved, not smoothed over.
- **Scope of this document.** Foreword only. Each subsequent chapter will carry its own notes file; shared conventions established here are not re-litigated downstream.

## 2. Governing philosophy

The translation aims at what might be called **confessional-historical fidelity**: the English should read as a nineteenth-century Dutch Reformed polemicist speaking in a register a contemporary Anglophone reader can actually inhabit, without flattening Kuyper's theology, his Dutch-Reformed sociolect, or the historical contours of his world.

Four commitments, in order of priority:

1. **Theological precision over idiomatic smoothness.** Where a technical term carries dogmatic weight (*Gemeene Gratie*, *Cor Ecclesiae*, *kleyne luyden*), the English must preserve the concept even at the cost of a slightly foreign surface.
2. **Voice over vocabulary.** Kuyper's characteristic triadic cadence (*zoo… zoo… zoo…*), his rhetorical apostrophe, and his sudden shifts from historical narrative to first-person confession are load-bearing. They are preserved even when standard English prose would prefer subordination.
3. **Historical texture over modernization.** The Foreword is a memoir of the 1860s–70s written in 1916. Anachronistic clean-up (e.g., "evangelical revival" for *Réveil*, "ordinary people" for *kleyne luyden*) would erase the very historical layering Kuyper is appealing to.
4. **Scholarly usability.** The English should be citable alongside Bratt (1998), Mouw, and the Kuyper Translation Society conventions — not a rival dialect.

## 3. Lexical decisions

Terms marked **kept** are left in Dutch or Latin, italicized on first occurrence, and glossed either parenthetically in-text or in a footnote. Terms marked **rendered** are translated, with the Dutch preserved on first occurrence where the Dutch itself is a term of art.

| Dutch / Latin | Treatment | English | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Réveil* | kept | — (footnoted) | Proper-noun movement label in the historiography (Da Costa, De Clercq, early Groen). "Evangelical revival" collapses it into the Anglo-American Great Awakening tradition, which is precisely what Kuyper is distinguishing his Calvinism from. |
| *kleyne luyden* | rendered + kept | "the *kleyne luyden* — the 'little people'" | Follows Bratt / Mouw / Harinck. "Common folk" and "common people" were rejected: *folk* in English carries a populist / Völkisch resonance alien to Kuyper's sixteenth-century citation of William of Orange, and *common* prejudges the question of *Gemeene Gratie*. |
| *Gemeene Gratie* | rendered + kept | "*Gemeene Gratie* (Common Grace)" | Established English term for Kuyper's doctrine. Dutch preserved on first occurrence because it is also the title of the three-volume work being cited. |
| *Staatkunde* / *Staatsrecht* | kept | — (explained in-text) | Kuyper's closing paragraph *turns on* the distinction between these two terms — practical political science vs. comprehensive constitutional law. English "statecraft" / "constitutional law" would obscure the argument. |
| *Nadere Toelichting* | kept | "*Nadere Toelichting* ('Further Explanation')" | This is a book title, not a descriptor. Kept in Dutch with a parenthetical gloss. |
| *Hervormde* / *Gereformeerde* | kept | — (italicized) | Both words translate as "Reformed" in English, but after the 1886 Doleantie they name two different Dutch churches. Collapsing them into a single English term would destroy the ecclesiological point. |
| *Blauwboekjes* | rendered + kept | "the Blue Booklets (*Blauwboekjes*)" | Concrete historical artifact (the blue-wrapper Canons-of-Dordt editions). English gloss first, Dutch in parentheses, full note in the apparatus. |
| *Gezelschappen* | rendered + kept | "the old 'Societies' (*Gezelschappen*)" | Technical term for the conventicle tradition. "Fellowships" was considered and rejected as overly ecumenical-modern. |
| *Staatschen* | rendered + kept | "the Statists (*Staatschen*)" | Kuyper's sixteenth-century opponents of Calvinistic ecclesiology. "Statists" is awkward but preserves the polemic. |
| *Dooperschen* | rendered | "Anabaptists" | Standard English equivalent. No loss. |
| *Cor Ecclesiae* | kept | — (footnoted on later occurrence) | Kuyper's own Latin phrase for the doctrine of Election as "the heart of the Church." Translating destroys the allusive register. |
| *Quousque tandem?* | kept | — (footnoted) | Cicero, *In Catilinam* I.1. Kuyper uses it rhetorically and expects the reader to catch the joke. |
| *Wat vermeet ge U!* | rendered | "How dare you!" | Idiomatic indignation. Literal "What do you presume!" would read as archaic translationese. |
| *het kindeke met het waterhoofd* | rendered (italicized) | "*The child with the hydrocephalus*" | Dutch idiom for the embarrassing product kept out of public view. Preserved as italicized calque because Kuyper is *using* the awkwardness of the image for self-deprecating effect. |
| Ivan Lasky / Johannes à Lasco | both preserved | as in source | Kuyper uses the Dutch transliteration first, then the Latinized form. Both are kept; the footnote supplies the Polish original (Jan Łaski) for scholarly lookup. |

## 4. Scripture and Psalm handling

- **1 Corinthians 1:26.** Rendered *per Kuyper's Dutch paraphrase*, not imported from the KJV, ESV, or NIV. Kuyper's "gij ziet uwe roeping, broeders, dat niet vele wijzen, niet vele machtigen, niet vele edelen zijn" is closer to the Statenvertaling than to modern English versions, and the argumentative work in the paragraph depends on the triadic rhythm (*wise / powerful / noble*) which Kuyper is borrowing forward into his description of Groen's political isolation.
- **Psalm citations.** Kuyper quotes the *berijmde Psalmen* — the Dutch metrical Psalter of 1773 — not the prose text. These are preserved as **metrical verse**, line-broken with `<br>` inside block quotes. The four citations are, in order: Psalm 25, Psalm 25, Psalm 89, and Psalm 89. They function liturgically in Kuyper's argument — sung confession as the soul of the *kleyne luyden* — and flattening them to prose would erase that function.
- **Hebrews-style doxological close.** The final Psalm 89 quotation is set off by Kuyper as rhetorical climax ("To flow out, at last, into the holy rapture"). The English matches the vertical break.

## 5. Voice, cadence, and syntax

- **Triadic *zoo… zoo… zoo***. Kuyper's signature cadence is preserved as "So it was here in our land, so it was in Scotland under Knox, so it had been in America under the Pilgrim Fathers." Standard English editing would prefer a single "so… and… and…" construction; that editing is refused.
- **Long periodic sentences.** Kuyper writes in long, hinged sentences with multiple subordinate clauses. They are kept long. Breaking them into short English sentences makes him sound modern and journalistic, which he is not.
- **First-person shifts.** The Foreword moves without warning between autobiographical ("I had never read a word of Groen van Prinsterer") and theological-historical registers. Those shifts are preserved as they fall in the Dutch.
- **Capitalized abstractions.** Kuyper's capitalized *Religie*, *Politiek*, *Staat*, *Revolutie* are rendered with English capitalization where the Dutch capitalizes; elsewhere lowercased per modern English convention.
- **Em-dashes.** Kuyper uses long dashes for parenthetical emphasis. Rendered as em-dashes (—), not en-dashes or parenthetical commas.

## 6. Historical-contextual glosses

Eleven footnotes are supplied to the Foreword, keyed by Markdown footnote syntax (`[^1]` … `[^11]`) for compatibility with Substack, GitHub Pages, Pandoc, and most static-site generators. The notes cover:

1. Cicero, *In Catilinam* (the *Quousque tandem* allusion).
2. Jan Łaski / Johannes à Lasco, with the Polish original restored.
3. Groen van Prinsterer and *Ongeloof en Revolutie*.
4. The 1853 Papal restoration of the Dutch episcopal hierarchy (the *Aprilbeweging*).
5. The *Nachtschool* / "Night School" pejorative.
6. The 1789 vs. 1578 periodization (French Revolution vs. Dutch Revolt).
7. The *Réveil* movement.
8. The *Blauwboekjes* and the Canons of Dordt / Five Articles.
9. The Compromise of Nobles (1565–66) and William of Orange's *kleyne luyden*.
10. Schleiermacher, *Gefühl*, and the Dutch Ethical tendency.
11. The *het kindeke met het waterhoofd* idiom and its application to *Nadere Toelichting*.

The footnotes aim at **the reader who is fluent in English, literate theologically, but not specialist in Dutch Reformed history**. They are not exhaustive; they are the minimum needed to follow Kuyper's argument without a reference shelf.

## 7. Names, places, and institutions

- **Personal names.** Dutch names are kept in their Dutch form: *Groen van Prinsterer*, *de Vries*, *Scholten*, *Gewin*, *Dibbets*, *Bechthold*, *Kühler*, *Grosheide*, *Comrie*. No Anglicization.
- **Place names.** *Leiden*, *Utrecht*, *Amsterdam*, *Beesd*, *Dordt* — Dutch forms. *Dordt* is preserved over *Dordrecht* because Kuyper uses the short form and because "Dordt" is the standard English-theological shorthand.
- **Institutions.** *Réveil*, *Aprilbeweging*, *Compromis der Edelen* kept in original; *Hervormde Kerk* / *Gereformeerde Kerken* kept in original (see §3).
- **Foreign scholars.** Alexander Schweizer of Zurich, *Glaubenslehre* — German kept, not translated as "Doctrine of Faith," because it is a book title.

## 8. What was deliberately *not* done

- **No modernization of theology.** Kuyper's "modernism" (the Leiden theological movement associated with Scholten) is **not** rendered as "modernist theology" or "liberal theology." It is a specific Dutch phenomenon with its own trajectory.
- **No inclusive-language revision.** "Brethren" in the 1 Cor 1:26 quotation is kept as *broeders*; "the man of Tarsus" is kept; William of Orange's *kleyne luyden* is not softened.
- **No footnote inflation.** Allusions Kuyper clearly expected his reader to catch (e.g., Owen, Comrie, Knox) are **not** footnoted in the translation body. Readers who don't know these names can look them up in §11 below; over-glossing would patronize the text.
- **No silent emendation of Kuyper's historical claims.** Where Kuyper's dating, attribution, or characterization is contestable (e.g., the framing of the *Réveil*, the role of Groen on his deathbed), the English renders Kuyper's claim as he made it. Scholarly correction belongs in the apparatus, not the text.

## 9. Formatting conventions for the repo

- **File.** `foreword.en.md` for the translation; `foreword.nl.md` for the Dutch source; `foreword.translator-notes.md` for this document.
- **Footnote syntax.** Markdown `[^N]` inline, `[^N]: …` definitions appended after a `---` rule. Compatible with Substack paste, GitHub rendering, Pandoc, mdBook, and Hugo.
- **Emphasis.** Italics via `*…*` for Dutch / Latin terms and book titles; bold reserved for structural emphasis only, never for scriptural citation.
- **Block quotations.** `>` prefix; metrical Psalm lines use `<br>` for line breaks inside a single block quote (so each Psalm renders as one logical quotation, not several fragments).
- **Em-dash.** Unicode `—` (U+2014), no spaces. No ASCII `--` in final output.
- **Smart quotes.** Curly `" " ' '` throughout.

## 10. Known limitations and open questions

- **Psalter identification.** The four Psalm citations are from the 1773 Dutch metrical Psalter, but Kuyper does not cite stanza numbers. The footnotes for the chapter body (not yet written) will supply Psalm number + stanza; the Foreword leaves them as Kuyper quotes them.
- **Schweizer's *Glaubenslehre*.** The 1844 edition is cited; later editions (1847, 1863) differ. Kuyper is recalling from memory half a century after the fact. The English does not correct him.
- **"Owen."** Kuyper refers to "Owen" without initial or date. Almost certainly John Owen (1616–83); possibly intended as the intellectual ancestor of Alexander Comrie's rejuvenation effort. No footnote is added in the body because the identification is inferential.
- **The Dibbets / Bechthold / Kühler / Grosheide triumvirate.** These are minor figures in Amsterdam Reformed life c. 1870. Biographical glosses are deferred pending archival work.
