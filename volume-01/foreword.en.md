# Foreword

In the autodidact there is always a streak of the daredevil; and since in the six years of my Leiden studies I never once attended a single lecture in law, I can already hear the *"Quousque tandem?"* that will likely be hurled at me from the classical juristic quarter, in the form of: "How dare you!"[^1]

Yet there is no presumption at issue here.

It is my unforced life-course that, with an urgency no resistance could withstand, has compelled me, more than it has enticed me, to write this work. My studies had initially moved in no way toward the political side. So far as scholarly engagement drew me at all, my love from the outset belonged to history, and within that historical field especially to Church History. Professor Dr. M. de Vries, who as a teacher at the Leiden Gymnasium had often given me kindling hints, continually pointed me to Church History as the field of study best suited to me, and Professor Dr. J. H. Scholten guided me in the same direction. From Scholten's side in particular a certain orientation came to me. For when I compared the modern Scholten with the orthodox professors at Utrecht, a striking phenomenon presented itself: Scholten's writings offered an overflowing recollection of Calvinistic literature, while among the orthodox professors the scarcity on that score struck one again and again. Scholten moved here in Holland along the same line as Dr. Alexander Schweizer in Zurich. In his *Glaubenslehre*, which appeared in 1844 from Orell, Füssli & Co. in Zurich, Schweizer had followed the method of suggesting, at each chapter, by citations from Calvin, Bullinger, Hyperius, Peter Martyr, Alsted and others, the historical continuity of his own modernism with the ancient Reformed theology. Determinism was slipped in for predestinarianism, and the identity was found. Since at that time I myself felt most strongly drawn to modernism, and yet from my upbringing cherished a warm sympathy for what our fathers had stood by in the sixteenth century, Schweizer and Scholten naturally attracted me most. Even so, my decisive life-choice came only through a prize-question of the Groningen theological faculty, to which I submitted an answer that was judged worthy, and that alone brought me to a firm decision. I had paid no attention at all to that prize-question, and it was Professor de Vries who pressed me to risk an answer. That prize-question concerned the doctrine of the Church held by Calvin and by the Polish scholar Ivan Lasky, commonly called Johannes à Lasco.[^2] So I came of myself to Calvin; and since Church and State in the sixteenth-century conception hung so intimately together, even then my studies drew me, up to a point, onto political terrain.

Even so, all contact with political life was still lacking. I remained a theologian and nothing more, and as yet felt not the slightest impulse to revive what in the sixteenth century had animated our fathers in the political sphere. I had never read a word of Groen van Prinsterer,[^3] and my political leaning declared itself most strongly when, in 1853, I flared up quite literally for the action against the Bishops' Hierarchy.[^4] Even at Beesd, no other action proceeded from me as a minister than one of a conservative tendency. It was nothing other than contact with the life still stirring in the so-called "Night School"[^5] that made me see the impossibility, on the one hand, of separating theology and politics from each other, and, on the other, of choosing 1789 instead of 1578 as the starting-point of the historical-political line.[^6] It was not the inspirations of the Réveil,[^7] nor the Supranaturalistic inventions, that gave me this contact with the historical life. This contact came out of the remnant of the old "Societies" (*Gezelschappen*), with their broad following. In those Reformed circles there was still a literature of their own, and that literature still spoke theology and politics as inseparably one. Only, the theological-political life in those circles appeared to be ageing. What Owen had kindled there as the clearest light had passed out of its time; and though Alexander Comrie had tried to rejuvenate it, even he had gone too little along with his time. Dibbets, Bechthold, and Kühler, the triumvirate at Amsterdam in those days who, together with Grosheide, were still striving to save our hardy Reformed, said it again and again: there had to be a clarifying of insight and, above all, a rejuvenated application, if the core of our people was to retain its inner strength undiminished even in these newborn conditions. For our Calvinistic people has never been satisfied with edifying exhortation. It has never been able to rest until it felt itself strong in a life- and world-view of its own, such as lies traced in broad outline in God's Word. Already in the days of Dordt, this core of our people was not merely pious in disposition but at the same time shaped, in a thoroughgoing sense, both theologically and politically. Read and re-read the Blue Booklets (*Blauwboekjes*) that came out in those days, blow upon blow.[^8] Genuinely theological, their writers felt themselves at home in the most probing theological questions of their day. The five articles that Dordt added to our Confession went so deeply into the root of the theological problems that by the eighteenth century most members of the *Hervormde* church understood next to nothing of them any longer, and therefore never opened them again; but in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries these weighty questions were debated even in the canal-boat and in the public market. So it was here in our land, so it was in Scotland under Knox, so it had been in America under the Pilgrim Fathers.

Of all shallowness where the glory of God was concerned, those people had a heartfelt abhorrence. It was the soul-piercing Psalm-tones that then resounded into the depths of the devout heart:

> "Lord, ah, make me Thy ways known through Thy Word and Spirit,<br>Teach me how they lie, and whither Thou dost turn Thy steps."

Then again:

> "God's hidden fellowship is found by souls in whom His fear doth dwell.<br>The holy mystery is shown to His friends, according to His covenant of peace."

Or also:

> "How blessed is the people that hears Thy sounds;<br>They walk, O Lord, onward in the light of Thy divine countenance."

To flow out, at last, into the holy rapture:

> "Thou, yea Thou art their glory, the strength of their strength.<br>Thy free favor alone receives the honor;<br>We lift our heads and shall wear the crown of honor,<br>Through Thee, through Thee alone, for Thy eternal good pleasure.<br>For God is our shield in the battle-field of this life,<br>And our King is given us from the God of Israel."

The remnant of this old people, when I came to Amsterdam in 1870, was already partly melting away. At the universities the leading Orthodoxy hardly knew Calvinism any longer. The Réveil had pushed it aside as the "Night School," indeed had driven it out. Among the name-bearing theologians, men were too busy with the authenticity of the biblical books and with the critical sifting of the sacred text. Dogmatics proper slid away beneath the paperwork; and it was, in fact, a discovery for me when in 1873, together with the late Rev. Gewin, I went to a secondhand bookseller in Leiden and there found and purchased an entire library out of which the old Calvinism still spoke to me as in sepulchral language. Thus I came to the old literature, and above all, through Dibbets, to the heirs of this spiritual inheritance. This moved me then to adapt, through *Gemeene Gratie* (Common Grace), the old Calvinistic politics to our so-thoroughly-altered time. To this, the Reformed theology of Neo-Calvinism owed its rise; and once again, as of old, this Reformed theology now stands as a power in the land, both in the *Hervormde* Church, which is still groping for hygienic restoration, and in the *Gereformeerde* Churches, which broke with her.

But of itself it could not stop there. As Paul had found at Corinth and Rome, and our Calvinists had felt in the sixteenth century, Religion and Politics were simply not to be separated from each other. This held in Paul's days, it was no different in the days of the Calvinists, and just as then, so it presented itself now: "You see your calling, brethren, that not many wise are, not many powerful, not many noble." Prince William of Orange lamented it so deeply out of his disappointed heart: that the League of the Nobles had given him no support,[^9] while conquering strength had flowed to him almost solely out of the *kleyne luyden*, the "little people." And now again, almost everything high in name and power left Groen van Prinsterer standing alone, and it was essentially the small citizen and the sturdy farmer who still gave ear to his summons. In the days of Paul's apostolate it was the "science" of the Greek and Roman philosophers that held the leading of minds in hand, and that looked down contemptuously, indeed disdainfully, on what the man of Tarsus wrote. Place over against the contempt of that erstwhile "science" only the Epistle to the Romans, a little book, were you to issue it separately, of not yet a single printed folio, and ask yourself whether the life- and world-view Paul gave forth in it, in depth of conception and in illumination from a higher light, does not utterly overshadow everything the intellectuals of Rome and Greece then oracled forth. There was nothing strange, therefore, in the fact that a pithy core, such as of old in the first Christians, and three centuries ago in the Calvinists, arose once more in our day, and knew how to enter thoroughly into a theology that offered a life- and world-view grounded in God's Word, a theology from which one could learn, confess, heroically wrestle, and spiritually rejoice.

For this reason, Neo-Calvinism too had to begin by first establishing the believing people anew in their theological principles; but it could not and might not remain there. Calvinism is most certainly theological in origin, but it is just as decidedly political in character. This was already plain in the seventies of the last century. Scarcely had our Reformed confession begun to revive more richly, when a political striving emerged again of itself. Already in 1870 the first shoots were set out; in 1878 we received our Anti-revolutionary Program; and the *Toelichting* (Explanation) of that Program, which appeared in 1879, could of itself bring nothing other than Neo-Calvinistic politics. *Anti-revolutionary* was the broader name, but for the Netherlands it had from of old been a historical flowering of life out of the Calvinistic root. With the reviving of the ancient heroic spirit, the Calvinistic keynote was bound to return; something for which it cannot be regretted enough, that the Réveil, glorious as it was in itself, had not already sensed this at the beginning of the nineteenth century. It was Groen van Prinsterer who first saw it. His Archive-studies especially brought him to it; and although Groen only on his deathbed thankfully affirmed the fullness of the Reformed confession, with its chief glory in Election, — still, his historical sense led him much earlier to align himself with Calvinism, though not without reserve. What he had written down as confession in the regulations of Christian-National School Instruction was still too methodistic and omitted Election, the *Cor Ecclesiae*; but soon there resounded through the whole land his inspired summons, that in Calvinism origin and warrant were given for our constitutional liberties. The Ethical vessel drifted off, with Schleiermacher's pennant at the masthead, too far into German-Lutheran waters.[^10] The Réveil went on seeking its main strength in the practical-philanthropic. And only the Reformed core felt the urge, the need, indeed the necessity, to step forth with an all-encompassing, central world-view; one that, however, just as in Paul's days, could not ripen out of the ruling science; and that rather, under the opposition of that science, had to lift its head in the courage of faith. On the political field as well, therefore, our rising has not come from the "wise and noble," but once again, just as in the days of the man of Tarsus and of the Reformation, from the small and the quiet of the land. You have only to trace the struggle of our old Calvinists with the State-power, with the Statists (*Staatschen*), and with the Anabaptists (*Dooperschen*), to perceive at once how thoroughly Calvinism had then developed and matured, even in a political sense, among the "little people."

It was the need to rouse that same striving and purpose again, and where it was already awake, to lead it, that I sought to meet, first in *Ons Program* and later in *De Gemeene Gratie*, to some degree. Yet it could not stop there. *Ons Program* appeared already in 1879, when the struggle had only just begun, and I had as yet had no time to orient myself more deeply on the political field. For the defective and insufficient character of that first political writing I have long had an open eye; but amid the many things assigned to me as daily task, time was entirely lacking to go into it more deeply. Only when my failing hearing confined me more to my study did time for this become available; and it is this more generous disposal of my time that has enabled me to complete the work now appearing.

I would have preferred to keep for the two parts of which this work will consist the simple title *Nadere Toelichting* ("Further Explanation"), under which the first installment indeed appeared. But when definitive publication was at hand I was firmly advised against it. At least for the first part, it was judged, this title did not fit. *The child with the hydrocephalus must remain contraband.*[^11] On the good advice of many I therefore now adopted the title *Antirevolutionaire Staatkunde* (Anti-revolutionary *Staatkunde*). Not *Antirevolutionair Staatsrecht* (Anti-revolutionary Constitutional Law). The expression *Staatsrecht* would have been too comprehensive a concept: it would have required first an exposition of the general system of all Anti-revolutionary politics, and thereafter a separate treatment of the widely divergent forms that general system had assumed according to differences of country and age. This would have far exceeded the time at my disposal, and would have gone entirely beyond my autodidactic powers. *Staatkunde* by contrast sets no such high demands. This is a concept that takes the rich material more from its practical side, and as such can naturally limit itself to a study that sets forth our principle for the Netherlands and in this period. Not in the concept *Staatsrecht*, but in the concept *Staatkunde*, the ethnological-historical limitation is, of itself, already given.

## Footnotes

[^1]: Opening words of Cicero's First Oration Against Catiline (63 BC): *Quousque tandem abutere, Catilina, patientia nostra?* — "How long, O Catiline, will you abuse our patience?" Kuyper invokes the line self-deprecatingly, imagining trained jurists reproaching a theologian for trespassing on their field.

[^2]: Jan Łaski (1499–1560), Latinized as Johannes à Lasco, was a Polish Reformer and superintendent of the Stranger Churches in London under Edward VI. His *Forma ac ratio* (1555) shaped Reformed church polity in England, the Low Countries, and Poland. Kuyper's Dutch form "Ivan Lasky" is preserved here.

[^3]: Guillaume Groen van Prinsterer (1801–76): Dutch statesman, historian, and archivist of the House of Orange. His *Ongeloof en Revolutie* ("Unbelief and Revolution," 1847) argued that the French Revolution was at root a religious uprising against Christian civilization. Kuyper came to regard Groen as his political predecessor and, at Groen's urging, his successor in the antirevolutionary movement.

[^4]: In 1853 Pope Pius IX restored the Roman Catholic episcopal hierarchy in the Netherlands (the "April Placards"), provoking the *Aprilbeweging* — a mass Protestant protest movement. Kuyper, a sixteen-year-old gymnasium student, was swept up in the agitation — his earliest political awakening.

[^5]: *Nachtschool* — the pejorative name applied by nineteenth-century modernists, and later by the Réveil, to the surviving orthodox Reformed conventicles that met outside the established church, often literally after dark. The term carried the double charge of *underground* and *benighted*; Kuyper adopts it with defiant irony.

[^6]: 1789 — the French Revolution, the founding moment of what Kuyper's tradition simply calls *Revolutie*. 1578 — the Dutch Revolt reaching institutional footing in the years leading to the Union of Utrecht and the first Reformed synods. The choice between these two starting points is, for Kuyper, the whole political question.

[^7]: *Le Réveil* — the nineteenth-century Reformed evangelical revival in French Switzerland and the Netherlands, associated with Isaäc da Costa, Willem de Clercq, and the early Groen van Prinsterer. Kuyper admired its piety and philanthropic energy but regarded its failure to produce a comprehensive political theology as decisive for the Calvinistic cause.

[^8]: *Blauwboekjes* — cheap, blue-wrapper editions of the Canons of the Synod of Dordrecht (1618–19) and related confessional material, widely circulated among Dutch Reformed households in the seventeenth century. The Canons — the "Five Articles of Dordt," answering the Remonstrant controversy — were later summarized in Anglophone catechesis as the "Five Points of Calvinism." Kuyper's point is that in the 16th and 17th centuries these technical documents were not clerical property but common reading, debated "in the canal-boat and in the public market."

[^9]: The Compromise of Nobles (*Compromis der Edelen*), 1565–66: a confederation of some four hundred Dutch nobles who petitioned Margaret of Parma, regent of the Spanish Netherlands, for toleration of Protestant worship. It collapsed under pressure, a failure Prince William of Orange bitterly recalled in his letters. *Kleyne luyden* — literally "little people" — was William's own term for the ordinary Dutch believers whose resistance ultimately carried the Revolt.

[^10]: Friedrich Schleiermacher (1768–1834), widely called "the father of modern theology," grounded religion in *Gefühl* — the feeling of absolute dependence — rather than doctrine. The Dutch *Ethische richting* (Ethical tendency) took up his program in the nineteenth century, drifting, in Kuyper's reading, away from Reformed confessional moorings into a German-Lutheran current.

[^11]: *Het kindeke met het waterhoofd moet contrabande blijven* — a Dutch idiom for something malformed or embarrassing that must be kept out of sight. Kuyper applies it self-deprecatingly to his original 1879 title *Nadere Toelichting* ("Further Explanation"): too modestly shaped for the definitive work now appearing under the title *Antirevolutionaire Staatkunde*.
